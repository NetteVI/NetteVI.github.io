Clock:in Haxe Game

A short story-based puzzle game developed in Haxe by a team of 4 during our Games Capstone course. Learn a bit more about how this course improved our skills in all areas of game-making (prototyping, evidence-based iteration, cross-platform publishing, art and sound).

You'll see how it really isn't that easy to wake up for a corporate office job every day - at least not with these clocks.

University of Washington
April 2018 - June 2018

Details
Capstone Course Details

The 10-week course was divided into approximate sections:

  • Week 1: Brainstorm ideas, form teams
  • Week 2-3: Paper prototypes
  • Week 4: User test and iterate
  • Week 5-6: Code game
  • Week 7-10: Test online, collect data, iterate, present (1x each week)

During the online phase, we published or promoted our game on various platforms such as on Facebook, Reddit, Itch.io, Albino Black Sheep, Newgrounds and Kongregate. The focus was collecting data on user retention and level completion during each iteration (and using methods such as A/B testing) and making measurable improvements in these areas.

Game Details

The game follows Emma, a corporate office worker returning from vacation. Each night, she has to set the alarm on a puzzling clock in order to get to work on time. Running out of time, or incorrectly setting the alarm negatively affects the story. Various puzzles keep things interesting for Emma (see the Images tab), but during the day, the game plays as a top-down simulator where the user decides what their office time should be spent doing.

Some features we found important to include were power-ups such as additional time and hints to help struggling players. These were able to be discovered during the story-based office levels as well, rewarding people who explored the optional story. Relationship levels are also tracked and allow for further power-up opportunities. A large part of the polishing done during the final few weeks was focused on puzzle difficulty balancing with these in mind.

The program itself was coded in Haxe using Visual Studio. While we only published the HTML5 version, the game also runs on Mac and Windows.

My Contributions

Each team member was responsible for designing and coding 1/4 of the games puzzle levels. We assisted and debugged across the whole project, but we were mainly responsible for the correctness of our own levels. There were many other tasks however, including story, art, sound, publishing, data collection, class presentations, etc. One of my specific roles was acting as the main artist.

Snapchat 3D Face Model and Avatar Driving

A complete rebuilding of the 3D facial model behind the Snapchat app, exploring how to express extreme emotions and realism. The 3D model is what allows the app to realistically mask and morph users' faces with filters and lens, as seen in the well-fitting and well-lit AR lens that were exclusively released for the iPhone X.

Want to be a talking wolf? Or swap your friend's face in over yours? This project worked to make that a reality. Or at least a bit closer to reality.

Worlds Apart 3D-Animated Short Film

Developed over one short year by a class of 24 amateur animators, Worlds Apart is truly a product of student sweat and tears. I co-led the Effects team for this 7 minute film, and was also a member of the motion animation, rigging, lighting, and rendering/composite teams.

It's a sweet story about imagination and heroics. But it's eye-opening to see what goes into making a film come to life.

University of Washington
September 2016 - June 2017

Details
Capstone Course Details

The course series is run each year by Barbara Mones in the Computer Science department, taking only a select number of students from an application process that includes a portfolio review. Nonetheless, the course also prioritizes students from multiple disciplines, and that lack formal experience in animation.

General Timeline:

  • June - August: Optional Story class. Year's story is decided from student ideas.
  • September - November: Course teaches each step of the animation pipeline.
  • December - January: Preproduction (Animatic, set layouts, story refinement)
  • by March: Motionmatic complete, Fresh Eyes showing to get new feedback
  • by June: Completed film (lit, rendered, with sound and fx)
  • Continuing year: Touch-ups and polish by student volunteers, before presenting the film at festivals

Technologies were mostly Autodesk Maya 2017.5 and Adobe Creative Suite.

After the introductory course in November, students are placed into variuos production teams that suit their skills and interests. The teams included:

Concept Art, Layout, Modeling, Rigging, Texturing/Shading, Motion, Facial Expressions, Lighting, Rendering/Composite, Look and Feel, Effects, Scripting, Editing

Progress Videos shows the film at various stages. More information about the course can be found at the UW Animation Research Lab website and the Inside the Process page. The film itself (and any scenes not yet public) cannot be shared online as it has not yet been screened at festivals.

My Contributions
In the above list, I was a member of the bolded production teams. Everyone worked on a variety of shots in different areas, and we all participated in the daily meetings where we would give feedback and present progress. I sketched out rough motion and polished fine motion for various shots. I also co-led the Effects team, which involved responsibilities such as arranging meetings, motivating team members (or removing them when they had more important film responsibilities than effects), and coordinating the effects demo reel which we presented to the director (our instructor) in March.

While we demo-ed many possible effects, only a couple made it into the film. One such is an emotional, tear-filled scene (right video). In the most recent iterations that polished and refined the film after the course ended, I was responsible for lighting the sequence of fantasy shots that occur in the middle of the film (see examples below).

Rendering/Composite went hand in hand with many of the lighting responsibilties. After lighting, I also composited and edited these same shots in Adobe After Effects.

Some of my rigging responsibilities included rigging the joint-based skeleton in some of the fantasy animals that can be seen in this cage scene (and the final scene).

Overall, the entire film was a large team effort and collaboration and iteration were essential to our success.

Adobe Spark Intelligent Website Design

A project prototype that explored intelligent website creation tools. Users have more than just products or personal projects they want to share; they have stories. And it should be as easy as just uploading their content - then our tools should lay it out and automatically design the site for them, based on the needs and the type of media that's been provided.

This is the idea I prototyped, beginning from the basics of the existing Spark Pages, but with the goal of 'more magic'.

Adobe Software Development Internship
June 2016 - September 2016

Details

This internship balanced ideas of UX design and artifical intelligence, and gave me experience in working with different teams in different areas. My focus was to build a large-scale prototype to test and demo ideas for this early-stage project.

To understand of the goal of the project, consider deliberate creators. As a case, consider specifically, 'Melissa'. She has been baking pies and pastries for years.

She:
  • + wants to share her work with a wider audience by creating a website
  • - is often busy handling her business
  • - lacks previous design or coding experience

Melissa wants a website design that fits to the content that she wants to publish.

In a standard workflow, this is what happens:

  1. Melissa provides background details about her needs
  2. She chooses a suggested template
  3. and then, she struggles to fit her content into a generic, inflexible template

Our project addresses what Melissa needs. An intelligent website system that can adjust to any variety of content to produce beautiful and responsive designs.

Technologies used included Javascript, Vue.js, CSS and HTML5.

Run Short Film using Primitive Graphics

The final project in our Introductory Graphics Course. In 2 weeks, my partner and I scripted, animated, edited and did everything in between to create this 1 minute short film. It was entirely created in C++ and OpenGL, using the programs and concepts that we extended in our previous assignments. But don't expect any high-poly models or fancy facial animation - we did it all in primitives and spline curves.

Inspired by Masuda Toshio's eerie music; come follow this short chase.

University of Washington
March 2016 - June 2016

Details
Project Details

The course website: https://courses.cs.washington.edu/courses/cse457/16sp/

I took this intro course in Spring 2016, and found it to be my favorite course in the entire department, motivating me to then pursue Computer Animation the following year. I also became the Teacher Assistant for this course in Spring 2017, when we decided to overhaul the project codebase, updating all assignments to OpenGL 4.1 and to be cross-compatible.

The course covers a wide variety of topics as can be found on the course syllabus, but it contains 4 main projects:

  1. Impressionist - image manipulation, OpenGL primitives, user-interface design, and image processing
  2. Modeler - 3d surfaces, surface normals, texturemapping, hierarchical modeling, and shading
  3. Trace - a ray tracer (geometry intersections, shadow attenuation, reflection/refraction)
  4. Animator - extends a spline-based animation system to support multiple curve types, and implements a particle system simulation engine

Each project is completed in a team of 2, and after completing the coding portion, we also use our program to create an artifact. For the 4th project, we actually utilize the model created in project 2. Run, this short animation, was produced in two weeks after completing Project 4: Animator. Among the class, it was voted 1st place. See the ranking and all the films here.

Technologies used: C++, OpenGL, FLTK UI toolkit, Adobe Photoshop and Adobe Premiere.
